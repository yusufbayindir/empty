We are building an iOS-native, AI-powered household finance app built explicitly for couples and shared-living arrangements — the first app to combine Copilot-quality design, Monarch's dual-login household architecture, Honeydue's granular privacy controls, and a proactive AI Spending Coach that initiates forward-looking nudges before overspending occurs rather than reporting it afterward. The market window is open: Mint's March 2024 shutdown displaced approximately 22 million users, no existing app fully serves the couples segment, and LLM and bank-linking infrastructure are now cheap enough for a solo developer to execute. Finance/Money scored 46/60 in our category evaluation — a 7-point lead over the next-best category — and the couples whitespace within it represents an unoccupied position with proven willingness to pay up to $100/year.

## 3. Winner Selection

**Selected Category:** Finance / Money  
**Specific App Type:** AI-Powered Household Finance App for Modern Couples and Shared-Living Arrangements  
**Score:** 46/60 — a 7-point lead over Health & Wellness (39), the next-best option

### Why Finance / Money Wins

**Market size and proven willingness to pay.** The personal finance app market is large ($4.8B+ addressable) and, critically, its users have already demonstrated subscription willingness to pay. YNAB sustains 75% 12-month retention — the highest of any app category — at $99/year. Copilot commands $95/year with 4.8 stars and 24,000+ ratings. These are not hopeful projections; they are proven comps with publicly observable outcomes.

**The Mint displacement event.** Mint's March 2024 shutdown created a once-in-a-decade demand shock: approximately 22 million users lost their primary budgeting tool simultaneously. Research indicates 20–30% of those users were couples or shared-household users seeking collaborative tools that the available alternatives (Monarch, YNAB, Copilot) do not serve well. This displaced demand pool is still actively seeking a solution in 2026.

**Why Now: Three converging forces.**
1. The Mint gap is real and the couples sub-segment remains unserved by any premium iOS app
2. LLM APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic) are now cheap enough that a solo developer can embed genuine AI financial coaching without proprietary ML infrastructure
3. Bank-linking infrastructure (Plaid, MX, Finicity) is mature, documented, and priced at indie-developer scale — this was not true in 2019 when Honeydue launched

**The differentiation opportunity is specific and buildable.** A score of 8/10 on Differentiation Opportunity reflects the fact that the gap is not "build a better budgeting app" (impossible against incumbents) but rather "build the first couples-first, proactive-AI, iOS-native household finance app" — a specific niche that no existing well-funded competitor has occupied. Copilot is solo-only. Monarch is retrospective. Honeydue is frozen. The whitespace is defined, documented, and occupiable by a single focused developer.

### Why Other Categories Were Rejected

- **Productivity (35):** Brutally saturated by ChatGPT, Notion, and well-funded AI productivity tools. New apps launched in 2025+ account for only 3% of subscription revenue in the category.
- **Health & Wellness (39):** Apple launched its own Health+ AI Coach in 2026, directly threatening the coaching segment. Competing against Apple as a solo developer is not a viable strategy.
- **Social / Community (28):** Winner-take-all network effects. Meta, TikTok, and Snapchat make this categorically inaccessible to independent developers.
- **Shopping (27):** Requires supply chain relationships, affiliate infrastructure, or price-comparison data APIs at scale. Not a software problem a solo developer can solve.

## 4. Target User Profile

### Demographics

Millennials and Gen Z adults, ages 25–38, in established relationships — married couples, cohabiting partners, or roommates sharing material expenses. They earn variable or dual incomes totaling $80,000–$200,000/year combined. They have multiple accounts across different banks (checking at Chase, savings at Ally, credit card at Amex) and their finances are partially but not fully merged.

### Psychographics

They are comfortable paying for software subscriptions they trust. They already pay for Spotify, Netflix, iCloud+, and likely at least one productivity app. They care about design — they notice when an app feels cheap. They are iOS-committed: iPhone, AirPods, Apple Watch, iPad. They have tried managing money together and found that spreadsheets create resentment, separate apps create opacity, and shared apps feel invasive.

### The Pain They Live With

56% of couples report arguing over money. The arguments are almost never about values — they are almost always about information asymmetry: one partner does not know what the other spent, neither knows if they are on track for a shared goal, and the conversation about finances feels like an interrogation rather than a collaboration.

Existing tools fail them in specific, documented ways:
- **Mint was free, but it is gone.** They have not found a worthy replacement.
- **YNAB is too rigid.** Zero-based budgeting does not work when one partner is a freelancer with variable income.
- **Monarch is comprehensive but retrospective.** It tells them what they spent last month; it does not prevent overspending this month.
- **Copilot is beautiful but solo.** If one partner uses Android, Copilot is categorically unusable.
- **Honeydue is couples-native but frozen.** Bank sync breaks every two weeks, support is unresponsive, and no new features have shipped since 2022.

### A Day in Their Life

It is a Wednesday morning. Sarah opens her banking app and sees a $340 charge at a restaurant she does not recognize. She screenshots it and texts her partner Marcus: "What was this?" Marcus is in a meeting. The charge was a team dinner he expensed, which he will get reimbursed for, but the reconciliation conversation does not happen until Sunday. Meanwhile, their shared Amex is approaching a limit neither of them is tracking, because neither has a single dashboard that shows all accounts together. Their vacation savings goal exists as a note in Apple Notes. They have no idea if they are on track.

This is the problem. The opportunity is not a prettier chart — it is eliminating the Wednesday morning text.

## 5. Reference App Analysis: Copilot Money

**Developer:** Copilot Money, Inc.  
**Estimated Active Subscribers:** 150,000–200,000 (based on $6M Series A in March 2024 and continued growth)  
**Rating:** 4.8 stars | 24,000+ App Store ratings  
**Price Model:** $13/month or $95/year; no free tier; extended trials via promo codes; web access added December 2025  
**Recognition:** Apple Editor's Choice; Apple Design Awards 2024 Finalist

### What Users Love

**Best-in-class iOS-native design.** Built by ex-Apple designers, Copilot received an Apple Design Awards nomination not as a courtesy but because the product genuinely demonstrates what a first-party Apple app would look like if Apple built a budgeting tool. Smooth animations, haptic feedback on financial interactions, customizable emoji category chips, and SF Pro typography with generous whitespace. The app earns its $95/year purely on design quality before any financial features are considered.

**AI categorization that genuinely learns.** The ML engine trains on user corrections and reaches near-perfect accuracy within 2–4 weeks. Users cite this as the feature that eliminates the #1 friction point of every competing app: repetitive manual re-tagging of the same merchants week after week. The system surfaces a visible correction history, letting users watch the app improve — a trust-building loop no competitor has replicated.

**Deep Apple ecosystem integration.** Native widgets, Siri Shortcuts, Apple Watch support, Apple Card and Apple Card Savings Account tracking via FinanceKit. Copilot feels like an Apple app because it integrates at the OS level. The 2026 iOS 26 Liquid Glass redesign was supported on day one.

### User Pain Points

**Completely solo-user.** No couples mode, no household sharing, no multi-seat budgets. A couple where one partner uses Android literally cannot use Copilot together. This is not a missing feature — it is a categorical exclusion from the household finance market.

**Retrospective-only tracking.** Copilot shows you how you spent after the fact. There is no zero-based methodology, no forward-looking spend prevention, and no proactive AI nudges before overspending occurs. Users who want more than a beautiful spending dashboard hit a hard ceiling.

**No shared goals and limited investment features.** No sector breakdown, no zero-based assignment, no partner-aware features of any kind.

### Key UX Patterns

- **Swipeable tab navigation** across Dashboard, Cash Flow, Categories, Accounts, and Investments — each screen self-contained rather than nested
- **'Mark as Reviewed' transaction inbox** — turns daily transaction triage into an inbox-zero ritual that drives daily app opens; the single most effective daily engagement hook in the category
- **Subscription detection surface** that proactively surfaces recurring charges — a standalone screen, not buried in settings
- **Magic wand budget rebalancing** — one-tap AI suggestions for reallocating unspent budget across categories
- **Per-merchant AI rules** — assign a category once, propagate forever; the rule history is visible to the user as proof of learning

### Retention Mechanisms

- **Daily engagement loop:** 20% of users open the app 5–10 times per day; the transaction inbox creates pull similar to email triage
- **Design delight:** The app is genuinely enjoyable to open — an aesthetic moat that converts casual check-ins into daily habits
- **Sunk cost of AI training:** Every correction improves categorization accuracy; switching costs increase over time as correction history cannot be transferred
- **Apple ecosystem lock-in:** Deep FinanceKit, Siri, and Widget integrations create switching cost for iOS-committed users

### Design Style

Dark-first, high-contrast iOS-native aesthetic with rich data visualizations. Deep charcoal/near-black backgrounds with bold color-coded category chips in vivid greens, oranges, and purples. Typography is SF Pro with generous whitespace. Charts are smooth animated line graphs and donut charts. Feels premium — closer to a Bloomberg terminal designed by an Apple HIG team than a traditional finance app. Light mode available but dark mode is the signature look. The 2026 iOS 26 Liquid Glass redesign updated both modes with Apple's new material system.

### Design Elements to Borrow

1. The 'Mark as Reviewed' transaction inbox pattern — turns passive data into an active daily ritual with clear completion state
2. Per-merchant AI rule system with visible correction history — builds user trust by showing the app is learning
3. Subscription detection as a standalone recurring screen, not buried in settings
4. Haptic feedback on key interactions (category assignment, budget completion) — physical confirmation of financial actions builds emotional connection
5. Color-coded category chips with emoji customization — personal expression within financial data reduces cognitive friction
6. The overall design philosophy: build every screen as if it were a first-party Apple app, not a fintech product

## 6. Reference App Analysis: Monarch Money

**Developer:** Monarch Money, Inc.  
**Estimated Active Subscribers:** 400,000–600,000 (based on $12.6M ARR in 2025, $75M Series B at $850M valuation in May 2025, and 20x subscriber surge post-Mint shutdown)  
**Rating:** 4.9 stars | 71,000+ App Store ratings; 4.7 stars | 22,300+ Google Play ratings  
**Price Model:** No free tier; 7-day trial. Monarch Core at $99.99/year (couples included); Monarch Plus at $14.99/month  
**Recognition:** Wall Street Journal "Best Budgeting App"; Forbes "Best Mint Replacement"

### What Users Love

**Complete household financial aggregation.** Bank accounts, credit cards, loans, investments, and real estate in a single unified dashboard. Separate logins per partner. The closest thing to a true "household CFO dashboard" in any consumer app — this is the foundational couples feature Copilot does not have.

**Flexible collaborative budgeting.** Shared household budget with individual "fun money" categories. Transaction-level commenting so one partner can explain a charge asynchronously before the other partner asks. Role-based access patterns. The bi-weekly "money date" UX ritual is embedded in the product onboarding — Monarch schedules the financial conversation into the relationship rather than leaving it to user self-organization.

**AI Assistant with actual personalization.** Spending insights drawn from real transaction data rather than generic advice. The assistant answers questions about the user's actual data — not boilerplate.

### User Pain Points

**Retrospective, not preventive.** Monarch is outstanding at showing where money went but has no proactive AI system that warns couples before they overspend. The AI assistant answers questions but does not initiate. There is no Wednesday morning notification that says "you are on track to overspend dining by $180 this month."

**Subscription cost resistance with a short trial.** At $99.99/year with only a 7-day trial, users accustomed to Mint's free model experience sticker shock. Seven days is insufficient for couples to properly evaluate a shared-finance workflow — they need at least 2–3 weekly review cycles to feel the product's value.

**Investment tracking reliability complaints.** Connection issues with certain brokerages, limited analysis depth, and syncing delays are the most-cited issues on Reddit and in App Store reviews. Data confidence issues undermine the product's core promise of financial visibility.

### Key UX Patterns

- **Dual-login shared household model:** Each partner has separate credentials but sees the same consolidated household view — the foundational architecture for any couples app
- **Transaction-level commenting:** Either partner leaves a note on any transaction; replaces the "what was this charge?" text message with in-app asynchronous communication
- **Customizable dashboard with drag-and-drop widgets:** Each couple arranges net worth, spending breakdown, goal progress, and cash flow cards into their preferred layout
- **Goal visualization connecting daily spending to future milestones:** Progress bars that contextualize each spending decision against a shared savings target
- **Bi-weekly money date structure embedded in onboarding:** A scheduled financial conversation ritual built into the product experience

### Retention Mechanisms

- **Joint financial history lock-in:** 3–6 months of shared transaction history, categorization rules, and goal progress creates extremely high switching cost
- **Relationship accountability layer:** Two-login structure makes both partners implicitly accountable — one person's churn breaks the shared dashboard for both
- **Mint refugee loyalty:** Users who rebuilt their financial tracking in Monarch post-Mint are unlikely to switch again; migration fatigue is a durable retention force
- **Net worth tracking as emotional anchor:** Combined household net worth growing monthly creates a positive feedback loop that makes the subscription feel essential

### Design Style

Clean, modern, cross-platform design language optimized for web-first legibility adapted to mobile. Light mode dominant with updated dark mode. Color palette: deep indigo/purple as primary brand color with teal and green accents for positive indicators. Card-based dashboard layout with rounded corners, clear hierarchy, and generous whitespace. Charts prioritize readability over animation — clear bar graphs and line charts. Less visually opinionated than Copilot — more functional and data-dense, less emotionally delightful. The 2025 brand refresh improved contrast and reduced information density per card.

### Design Elements to Borrow

1. Dual-login shared household model — the foundational architecture that enables shared visibility without password sharing
2. Transaction-level commenting system — the killer couples feature; asynchronous partner communication about individual charges
3. Goal visualization that contextualizes daily spending against future milestones
4. Customizable dashboard widget system — high personalization creates ownership and reduces churn
5. Bi-weekly money date structure in onboarding — embed the financial conversation ritual into the product rather than leaving it to the user

## 7. Reference App Analysis: Honeydue

**Developer:** Honeydue, Inc.  
**Estimated Total Installs:** 500,000–1,000,000 historically (free app, broad distribution); active user base estimated at 100,000–200,000 given stagnation since 2022  
**Rating:** 4.5 stars iOS | 3.4 stars Google Play (significant platform quality disparity)  
**Price Model:** Completely free — optional voluntary tips ($0.99–$9.99/month), in-app advertising, affiliate commissions. Debit card program discontinued September 2023.

### What Users Love

**Per-account privacy granularity.** The most sophisticated privacy controls in any couples finance app. Each partner independently controls whether each individual account is fully shared (transactions + balances), balances-only, or fully hidden. This enables partial transparency without all-or-nothing disclosure — the architecture that makes the app psychologically safe for couples who are not fully financially merged.

**Zero cost with no paywalled features.** The only full-featured couples finance app that is completely free. This eliminates the single biggest adoption barrier in the category and makes Honeydue the default starting point for couples who are not yet ready to pay for a budgeting subscription.

**Couple-native communication features built from the ground up.** In-app chat with emoji reactions on specific transactions, bill reminder notifications sent to both partners simultaneously, and expense flagging for partner review. These features were designed for couple dynamics, not bolted onto a solo app.

### User Pain Points

**Effectively in maintenance mode as of 2026.** No new features since 2022. Debit card discontinued. Support emails go unanswered for months. The product shows the same pre-shutdown signals as the now-defunct Honeyfi/Firstly. Users are rightly nervous about the app's longevity, and the 3.4-star Google Play rating reflects high churn driven by this uncertainty.

**No AI, no goals, no investments, no forward-looking tools.** Honeydue does basic transaction visibility and bill reminders well, but the feature set is frozen circa 2020. No AI spending coach, no savings goal tracker, no net worth dashboard, no variable income budgeting mode.

**Chronic bank sync failures.** Accounts stop updating after 1–2 weeks, requiring manual reconnection. Customer support is unresponsive. No desktop or web interface. Mobile-only with unreliable sync is the single most complained-about issue in App Store reviews and Reddit threads.

### Key UX Patterns

- **Three-column transaction view:** Individual Partner A expenses / Individual Partner B expenses / Shared household expenses in parallel — instantly surfaces who is spending what without requiring a unified ledger
- **Per-account privacy toggle:** Three-option selector (full / balance-only / hidden) at the individual account level — not at the app level; the most granular privacy control in the category
- **In-app emoji chat on individual transactions:** Either partner reacts to or comments on any specific charge; lightweight communication without leaving the app
- **Shared bill calendar with dual-reminder system:** Upcoming bills trigger notifications to both partners simultaneously; eliminates the "did you pay the electric bill?" conversation

### Retention Mechanisms

- **Network effect between partners:** Both partners must be in the app for it to function — one person cannot churn without breaking the shared dashboard for both; the strongest structural retention mechanism in the category
- **Free pricing with no commitment:** Zero cost removes the active cancellation decision — couples stay by default rather than by choice
- **Bill reminder habit loop:** Recurring bill notifications create a dependency on the app for household coordination even when active transaction tracking lapses

### Design Style

Functional, minimalist mobile design prioritizing information clarity over visual delight. Light mode only (no dark mode). Muted color palette — whites and light grays with a coral/salmon accent for brand elements and green/red for income/expense indicators. No animations or haptics of note. The three-column partner view is the app's most visually distinctive element and immediately communicates its couples-first purpose. Overall aesthetic is closer to a clean utility app than a premium consumer product. No significant visual refresh since 2019.

### Design Elements to Borrow

1. Per-account privacy granularity (full / balance-only / hidden toggle): non-negotiable — implement at account level, not app level
2. Three-pane partner spending view: visualizing "your spending / their spending / shared spending" in parallel immediately communicates shared financial transparency without requiring a merged ledger
3. Transaction-level emoji reaction and comment: low-effort, high-value couple communication anchored to specific financial events
4. Dual-partner bill reminder system: both partners receive the same notification for shared bills — deceptively simple but eliminates one of the most common couple finance friction points

## 8. Gap Analysis

The three leading apps in this space each solve one or two dimensions of the couples finance problem well, but no single app solves all dimensions simultaneously. The combination of missing features across all three defines the entire opportunity space.

**Copilot** solves the design and AI categorization problem for solo users brilliantly but has zero couples functionality. It is the best finance app on iOS for an individual but is categorically unusable as a household tool. It has no shared dashboard, no multi-user login, no joint goals, and no partner-aware AI. It proves that premium design commands premium pricing ($95/year) and high daily engagement, but it has deliberately ignored the household market.

**Monarch** solves the household aggregation and couples visibility problem better than any other app, with dual-login architecture, transaction commenting, and customizable shared dashboards. But Monarch is a retrospective dashboard, not a proactive financial coach. Its AI assistant answers questions when prompted but never initiates. It shows couples where money went but does not warn them where money is going. There is no "you are 12 days from overspending dining by $200" alert. Its couples workflow also assumes full transparency, making it uncomfortable for couples who want selective account privacy.

**Honeydue** solves the couples-native communication and privacy granularity problem uniquely well. Its per-account privacy toggle is the most sophisticated privacy architecture in the category. Its three-pane view and in-app emoji chat are genuinely useful couple-specific UX innovations. But Honeydue is frozen in time: no AI, no goals, no investments, no proactive features, failing bank sync, and a product that appears to be in pre-shutdown maintenance mode. It proves that couples-first UX is a distinct and valuable product category, but its execution is now dangerously obsolete.

**The Gap:** The market has no app that combines:

1. Copilot-quality iOS-native design and AI categorization
2. Monarch's dual-login household architecture and goal visualization
3. Honeydue's per-account privacy granularity and couple-native communication patterns
4. A proactive AI spending coach that initiates forward-looking nudges before overspending occurs rather than reporting it afterward

Every app in the category is either solo-first, retrospective-only, design-poor, or in decay. The specific whitespace is: a beautifully designed, iOS-native, couples-first household finance app with proactive AI that warns before overspending — with granular privacy controls that allow partial transparency and a joint goal system that creates shared accountability without surveillance dynamics. No app on the market in 2026 occupies this position.

## 10. Design Inspiration for Designer Agent

This section provides specific, actionable design guidance derived from competitive analysis. The Designer Agent should treat this as a design brief, not a mood board.

### Overall Design Philosophy

**Build every screen as if it were a first-party Apple app.** This is not aesthetic advice — it is a product strategy. Copilot's $95/year price point and Apple Design Awards nomination are direct evidence that iOS-native design quality commands premium subscription pricing. Every animation, haptic, typography choice, and color decision should be evaluated against the standard of "would Apple ship this?"

### Visual Language

**Dark-first, high-contrast iOS-native aesthetic** (Copilot's influence). Deep charcoal/near-black backgrounds as the default. Bold color-coded category chips in vivid, distinct colors — not pastels, not muted tones. SF Pro typography with generous whitespace. Animated line graphs and donut charts, not static images. Light mode must be beautiful but dark mode is the signature experience.

Apply the 2026 iOS 26 Liquid Glass material system to navigation elements and modals. Support Dynamic Island on iPhone 15 Pro and later. Design for notch-free layouts on iPhone 16 series.

### Core Layout Patterns

**Three-panel household dashboard** (Honeydue's structural insight adapted to premium design quality):
- Left panel: Partner A spending summary
- Center panel: Shared household totals and joint goals
- Right panel: Partner B spending summary

This three-panel structure should be the primary dashboard metaphor. It immediately communicates the app's couples-first identity without requiring explanation.

**Customizable widget cards** (Monarch's pattern): Net worth, joint goal progress, cash flow, and AI digest cards should be drag-and-drop arrangeable on the dashboard. Personalization creates ownership.

**AI Spending Digest surface:** A dedicated weekly digest card on the dashboard — the proactive AI nudge surface. This should be visually prominent (not buried), use a distinct visual treatment (perhaps a subtle glow or animated border to indicate "new insight available"), and feel like a message from a trusted advisor, not a system alert. This is our most differentiated feature and it must feel intentional in the visual hierarchy.

### Specific UX Patterns to Implement

**From Copilot:**
- Mark as Reviewed transaction inbox — the primary daily engagement mechanism; give each transaction a clear reviewed/unreviewed state with satisfying completion animation
- Per-merchant AI rule system with visible correction history — show users the app learning; display "Trader Joe's has been auto-categorized as Groceries 47 times"
- Subscription detection as a standalone screen (not in settings)
- Haptic feedback on: category assignment, goal milestone reached, budget limit approached, partner's transaction reviewed
- Color-coded category chips with emoji customization

**From Monarch:**
- Dual-login architecture: each partner signs in separately with their own credentials; the app presents a unified household view
- Transaction-level commenting: tap any transaction to leave a note; both partners see the note in their transaction feed
- Goal progress bars that contextualize spending — "This $47 dinner moves your vacation fund back 2 days"
- Bi-weekly money date prompt embedded in the app (a scheduled check-in surface, not a push notification)

**From Honeydue:**
- Per-account privacy toggle: implement three states (Full / Balances Only / Hidden) at the individual account level; present this prominently in account setup, not buried in settings
- Dual-partner bill reminder: when a shared bill is due, both partners receive the same notification simultaneously
- In-app emoji reactions on transactions: lightweight partner communication anchored to specific financial events

### Suggested Screen Inventory

The following screens should be designed as a minimum viable scope:

**Onboarding Flow (couples-specific):**
1. Welcome + couples value prop screen
2. Partner A account linking (Plaid OAuth flow)
3. Partner invite: send invite link to Partner B
4. Privacy setup: per-account toggle for each linked account
5. First joint goal creation
6. Notification permission request
7. Dashboard introduction / tour

**Core App Screens:**
- Main Dashboard (three-panel household view with widget cards)
- Transaction Feed (with Mark as Reviewed states and partner comments)
- AI Spending Digest (weekly proactive nudge surface)
- Joint Goals (shared savings goals with individual contribution tracking)
- Budget / Cash Flow (variable income-friendly envelope method)
- Accounts (all linked accounts with privacy indicators)
- Subscriptions (standalone recurring charge detection surface)
- Expense Split (settlement tracking with Venmo/Zelle deep links)
- Partner Profile (invite, connection status, privacy settings per partner)
- Settings (subscription management, notification preferences, account management)
- Paywall / Upgrade screen (freemium conversion surface)

### Interaction Design Notes

- **Onboarding must complete in under 4 minutes for solo user; under 7 minutes for couple.** The partner invite step should feel low-friction — a single tap to send an SMS or AirDrop invite, not a form.
- **Privacy toggles must be understandable without explanation.** Use plain language ("Show everything" / "Show total only" / "Keep private") before revealing the technical labels.
- **The AI Spending Coach digest should read like a text from a smart friend, not a bank alert.** Warm, direct, specific. "You are on track to overspend dining by $180 — mostly at Shake Shack ($67) and three UberEats orders ($89)." Not: "Category variance detected: Food & Dining 23% above projection."
- **Joint goals should feel social, not transactional.** Show both partners' contribution history. Celebrate milestones. Use progress photography (user-set cover image for each goal) to make abstract savings targets feel real.

## 11. Monetization Strategy

### Pricing Tiers

**Freemium with 14-day free trial, then hard paywall.**

| Tier | Monthly | Annual | Seats |
|---|---|---|---|
| Solo | $7.99/month | $59.99/year | 1 |
| Couples / Household | $12.99/month | $99.99/year | 2 |

Annual plans are the primary monetization engine. RevenueCat 2026 data confirms annual plans dominate the Finance and Health categories. Default the paywall to the annual option with monthly as the secondary choice.

**Secondary revenue:** One-time AI financial report exports at $2.99 per report for free-tier users (annual spending summary, tax-prep categorization export, household net worth snapshot).

### Monetization Rationale

The $99.99/year couples tier is benchmarked directly against Monarch Core ($99.99/year) and below Monarch Plus ($14.99/month = ~$180/year). We are priced to win on value, not to compete on price. The couples tier generates $99.99/year for two seats — effectively $50/seat — which is below YNAB ($99/seat) and below Copilot ($95/seat). The household unit economics favor us because each couples acquisition produces two retained users at a single acquisition cost.

**Target LTV:** $120–180 per household annually, consistent with YNAB's proven model.

**Referral mechanic (structural virality):** Every Partner A who invites Partner B is a zero-cost second-seat acquisition. The couples onboarding flow should make partner invitation feel mandatory ("Set up your household" is the CTA, not "Invite your partner optionally"). This built-in viral loop is unavailable to solo-user competitors.

### Conversion Strategy

- **14-day trial** (not 7 days like Monarch — couples need more time to evaluate a shared workflow)
- **Paywall trigger:** After 14 days OR after linking more than 2 accounts, whichever comes first — the account-linking trigger catches power users before the trial expires
- **Couples tier upsell:** When Partner A is on the solo tier and Partner B accepts an invite, auto-present the couples tier upgrade with a "You're saving $16.98/month vs. two solo plans" framing

### Developer Economics Note

Bank-linking infrastructure costs must be budgeted explicitly. At Plaid's per-connected-account rates (~$0.30–$0.50/month per connection), a developer with 500 active users averaging 3 linked accounts each faces $450–$750/month in data costs before reaching subscription revenue threshold. Evaluate Plaid alternatives (MX, Finicity, direct OAuth bank connections) during the development phase and build cost-per-connected-account into the unit economics model before launch.
